Changes to the Female Body During the Menstrual Cycle

The menstrual cycle is a natural process that occurs in a woman's body, preparing it for the possibility of pregnancy. This cycle usually begins during adolescence and continues until menopause, which generally happens around the age of 52.

The menstrual cycle is defined by the duration from the first day of one period to the first day of the subsequent period, governed by a complex interplay of hormones. A regular menstrual cycle can vary widely among women, typically ranging from 21 to 35 days for adults, and 21 to 45 days for young teens. While a 28-day cycle is commonly cited as the average, it's merely a midpoint of the normal range and not necessarily the norm. Regularity is more accurately assessed by the predictability and consistency of the cycle length for each woman over time.

As part of the menstrual cycle, the uterus's lining thickens, and an egg, necessary for pregnancy, is produced in the ovary. The egg is eventually released from the ovaries during ovulation, which is also the time when a woman is most fertile (~5 days before ovulation, up through 1-2 days after ovulation). If the egg is not fertilized, pregnancy cannot happen. The uterus's lining will be shed during a menstrual period, after which the cycle restarts.

A period, another term for menstruation, is the regular discharge of blood and mucosal tissue that happens as part of the menstrual cycle. The bleeding and discharge of the mucosal lining of the uterus through the vagina usually lasts between 2 and 7 days. It happens during the menstrual phase, which is the first part of the menstrual cycle and begins when the egg from the previous cycle is not fertilized.

Typically, periods stop during pregnancy and do not resume during the early stages of breastfeeding. During menopause, a woman's periods stop for good. This usually happens between the ages of 49 and 52. Menopause is when a woman hasn't had a period in a year or more.

The Reasons for Menstrual Cycle Tracking

Tracking your menstrual cycle can be incredibly beneficial for various reasons, including understanding your unique patterns, predicting ovulation, improving your overall health and wellness, understanding your sex drive, and managing your mood.

Understanding Your Patterns

Though every woman is different, having an unpredictable period is more common than you think. Generally, a 28-day cycle is known as the global average but may not be your personal average. By being aware of your cycle, you will feel more in control and be less surprised by your next period.

Predicting Ovulation

Ovulation is the process of releasing an egg from the ovaries. It is the time when pregnancy is most likely to occur. By tracking your cycle, you can better understand your fertility and use this information to plan for pregnancy or other reproductive health decisions.

Improving Overall Health and Wellness

The menstrual cycle directly indicates a woman's overall health. So, it's said that periods are your body's way of telling you that things are working as they should. Tracking your cycle can help you identify any underlying conditions or irregularities that need to be addressed. Taking the time to log various details of your cycle allows you to recall important information when speaking with your healthcare provider.

Monitoring Sex Drive

Around ovulation, you may notice spikes in your sexual desire. By tracking your sexual drive and sexual activity, you can determine patterns in your cycle. Being aware of your sexual activity can help you understand when you might feel the most desirable or not.

Understanding and Managing Mood Swings

Hormonal changes throughout the menstrual cycle can cause changes in mood, such as irritability, anxiety, or feeling more affectionate. Learning when these changes happen can be another piece of information to help you understand the rhythm of your cycle and manage any symptoms that may arise.

It can also help women who have PMS, which can cause mood swings, cramps, bloating, and other symptoms.

Helping with Family Planning

Tracking ovulation can provide valuable insights into your fertility and help you understand when you are and aren't fertile. It is important to note that your fertile window, or when it's possible to get pregnant, is only several days long. This includes the few days leading up to ovulation and the day of ovulation.

This can help you plan your life accordingly and make informed decisions about family planning. Also, it can help women who are trying to get pregnant because it lets them know when their most fertile days are.

Ovulation Tracking as a Contraceptive

Moreover, ovulation tracking can be used as a contraceptive method, also known as the "fertility awareness method." This method involves tracking various biomarkers, such as basal body temperature, cervical mucus, and ovulation prediction kits, to identify the fertile days of a woman's menstrual cycle. In theory, you might be less likely to get pregnant if you can figure out when your fertile days are and stay away from sexual activity during those times.

However, it is essential to note that this method requires consistent and correct use. It may not be as effective as other methods of contraception, such as hormonal birth control or condoms. It's always recommended that you consult a healthcare provider to discuss the best contraceptive options.

Tracking Overall Health

Finally, by tracking ovulation, you gain valuable insight into the state of your overall health. Observing changes in your menstrual cycle can reveal the impact of various lifestyle factors or health conditions. By keeping a record of your cycle, you can see the long-term effects of any treatments or changes you make to your health.

This can be especially useful for women trying to conceive or for those managing a health condition such as Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS). By tracking your ovulation, you can gain a deeper understanding of your body and make more informed decisions about your health.

Closing Thoughts

Tracking the menstrual cycle and ovulation can help women learn more about their own unique patterns, find out when they are most fertile, and see how their health changes affect them.
